01 — Problem

Families are coordinating across too many disconnected tools.

Before defining the solution, I needed to understand exactly who was struggling and why existing tools weren't enough. The research revealed a more nuanced problem than I initially assumed.

Original Assumption

"Families just need a shared calendar app."

Before conducting research, I assumed the design problem was straightforward — build something clean and simple. That assumption didn't survive first contact with real users.

  • Tools like Google Calendar and Cozi exist but don't account for users with motor or cognitive differences
  • No existing app meaningfully serves both adults and young children in the same interface
  • Manual data entry is the biggest friction point — parents are too busy to maintain yet another app

Refined Problem Statement

"Dual-income parents of children under 12 spend significant time each week coordinating schedules across multiple disconnected tools — none of which were built for multi-user family contexts, users with accessibility needs, or children."

  • Specific primary user: dual-income parents, caregivers
  • Quantified friction: multiple apps, manual coordination overhead
  • Clear competitive gap: no existing solution covers accessibility + children together
  • Three distinct user types with fundamentally different needs, scoped into one product

How Might We

How might we design a scheduling experience that is effortless for busy adults, safe and engaging for children, and fully accessible to users of all abilities — without sacrificing simplicity for any of them?

02 — Research

What I learned from real users shaped every design decision.

I conducted competitive analysis, online surveys, and 6 semi-structured user interviews to understand what families genuinely needed — and where existing tools fell short.

6
User interviews conducted with parents aged 26–44
34
Survey respondents recruited via parenting communities
4
Competitor apps analyzed: Cozi, FamilyWall, OurHome, Google Cal
3
Distinct user types identified: parents, children, users with accessibility needs

Insight 01

Too Busy to Plan

Parents expressed that the overhead of manually inputting events into yet another app was a dealbreaker. The design needed to pull from existing tools automatically — not add to the data-entry burden.

"I already have three apps trying to do this. If I have to type everything in again, I'm just not going to use it." — Survey respondent, parent of 2, age 34

Insight 02

Hard to Learn New Tools

Participants — especially those managing neurodivergent children or living with their own disabilities — flagged that onboarding new technology was a significant barrier. Familiarity mattered more than novelty.

"My brain doesn't have bandwidth for learning new software right now. It needs to just work like the calendar I already use." — Interview participant, single parent, age 41

Insight 03

No Child-Friendly Options Exist

Parents of younger children (ages 5–10) wanted their children to be able to check their own schedule independently, but no existing calendar tool had a mode safe and readable for that age group.

"My 7-year-old asks me constantly what's happening today. I'd love for her to just look it up herself." — Interview participant, parent of 3, age 38

Insight 04

Navigation Overwhelm

Users with accessibility needs — including low vision and motor differences — stressed the importance of simple, visual information architecture. Too many steps, buttons, or words created friction that made apps unusable.

"Most apps assume I can just scroll through a wall of text. I need things to be visual and obvious." — Interview participant, user with low vision, age 29

03 — Users

Three users. One product. Different needs.

Research surfaced three distinct user archetypes. I designed FamILY to serve all three simultaneously — with parents as the primary user and accessibility as a non-negotiable constraint throughout.

👩
Jordan, 36
Working parent · Primary user

"I just need everything in one place so I'm not dropping the ball."

Goals

  • Coordinate two kids' activities without using 3 apps
  • Share schedule with co-parent seamlessly
  • Import events from work calendar automatically

Pain Points

  • Manual data entry feels like a second job
  • Forgets to update the family when plans change
  • Existing apps aren't designed for the whole family
🧒
Mia, 8
Child user · Secondary user

"I want to know if we're doing something fun today."

Goals

  • Check her schedule independently (without asking a parent)
  • Understand what's happening today in a visual format
  • Feel ownership over her own time and activities

Pain Points

  • Can't read dense text-based calendar UIs
  • Needs large touch targets and simple icons
  • Easily overwhelmed by too many options or steps
🧑‍🦽
Marcus, 44
Accessibility user · Constraint-setter

"Most apps just aren't built with people like me in mind."

Goals

  • Navigate a scheduling app without motor precision demands
  • Receive clear audio/visual feedback for every action
  • Use a screen reader without losing functionality

Pain Points

  • Small touch targets are inaccessible with tremors
  • Low contrast text is unreadable with low vision
  • Complex multi-step flows are exhausting to complete

04 — Design Process

From rough sketches to refined screens — what changed and why.

I moved through three fidelity stages. The designs changed substantially between lo-fi and mid-fi after early feedback revealed a fundamental mismatch between my initial vision and what users actually needed.

Design Fidelity Stages

01
Low-Fidelity Sketches

Quick hand-drawn concept sketches to explore layout ideas and navigation patterns. Used to gather early directional feedback before committing to any visual style.

02
Mid-Fidelity Wireframes

Grayscale digital wireframes built in Figma. This is where the core IA was validated — and where significant structural changes happened after user feedback sessions.

03
High-Fidelity Mockups

Full-color, interactive prototype. Color palette, typography, and component styles were tested and revised multiple times for both aesthetic appeal and WCAG AA contrast compliance.

The Pivot Moment

Before usability feedback, my instinct was to design a standard calendar grid with a few accessible elements layered on top. That assumption didn't hold.

Users with accessibility needs didn't just want a "more accessible" version of the same thing — they needed a fundamentally different navigation model. And children needed their own mode entirely, not just a simplified view of the adult interface.

This insight drove the creation of three distinct modes (Parent, Kids, Accessibility) sharing a common data layer — one of the biggest structural changes from lo-fi to mid-fi.

05 — Usability Testing

Six participants. Eight findings. Two rounds of iteration.

I ran two rounds of moderated usability testing — one at mid-fidelity and one at high-fidelity — with 6 participants including parents, a child user, and a user with accessibility needs. Participants completed 3 core tasks per session.

6 participants tested 2 rounds of testing 3 tasks per session Moderated remote sessions Tasks: Add event · Switch to Kids Mode · Set accessibility preferences
Critical Announcement text boxes looked like tappable buttons 5 of 6 users

Before (Mid-fi)

Announcement cards on the home screen used rounded corners, drop shadows, and a light fill — the same visual affordances as interactive buttons. Almost every participant tapped on them expecting a response.

Raised card with border-radius 12px, shadow, and colored fill — matched interactive button pattern exactly

After (Hi-fi)

Removed drop shadows and changed the shape to a flat, borderless layout with subtle background tinting. Added an explicit "Announcement" label in small caps. No more confusion with buttons.

Flat layout, no shadow, left-border accent only, small-caps label distinguishes from actionable elements
Critical Page redirects for event details broke navigation flow 4 of 6 users

Before (Mid-fi)

Tapping an event on the calendar navigated to a full separate screen. Users lost their place in the calendar and had to use the back button to return — adding friction and confusion, especially for accessibility users.

Full page navigation → calendar context lost → back-button required to return

After (Hi-fi)

Replaced full-page navigation with bottom-sheet overlays that slide up over the calendar. Users can review event details and dismiss without losing their calendar context.

Bottom sheet overlay slides up → calendar remains visible in background → single swipe to dismiss
Major Kids Mode color palette was overstimulating 3 of 6 users

Before

Initial Kids Mode used 6+ high-saturation colors simultaneously. Parents of children with sensory sensitivities flagged this immediately. The child user in testing took significantly longer to find targets on busy screens.

6 saturated colors, no dominant hierarchy, uniform brightness across elements

After

Reduced to 3 primary colors with clear visual hierarchy. Used color to distinguish event types (not decoration). Increased whitespace and reduced visual density by 40%.

3-color system, color = event category, whitespace increased, icon-first layout
Major "Add event" CTA was unclear in accessibility mode 2 of 6 users

Before

The floating action button (FAB) used a + icon only, with no text label. The accessibility user and one parent failed to identify it as the primary action during their first session.

Icon-only FAB → no accessible label → screen reader read "button" with no context

After

Added a text label "Add Event" to the FAB in accessibility mode. Added aria-label for screen reader support. Increased tap target size from 44px to 56px to meet WCAG 2.5.5 AAA target size guidelines.

Labeled FAB "Add Event" + aria-label + 56px tap target → 100% task success in round 2
Minor Mode-switching between Parent/Kids wasn't discoverable 2 of 6 users

Before

The mode toggle was housed in Settings, two taps from the home screen. One parent didn't find it at all during the first testing round.

Settings → Profile → Switch Mode → 3 taps from home screen

After

Surfaced mode switching to a persistent icon in the top navigation bar. Users can switch between Parent and Kids views in a single tap from anywhere in the app.

Top nav persistent icon → single tap to switch → animated transition indicates context change

06 — Outcomes

What changed because of the design work.

After two rounds of testing and iteration, measurable improvements across task success, learnability, and user satisfaction confirmed the design decisions were working.

88%

Task completion rate across all 3 core tasks in round 2 testing — up from 67% in round 1. The mode switch and FAB changes had the biggest individual impact.

4.0/5

Average ease-of-navigation score from post-test questionnaires. Participants described the interface as "clear" and "less overwhelming than I expected."

5/5

Participants successfully completed Kids Mode onboarding in round 2, compared to 3/5 in round 1. The simplified color palette and larger touch targets drove the improvement.

100%

Task success for the labeled FAB in accessibility mode during round 2 — up from 0% in round 1 when the icon-only button was used. A single labeling change eliminated the problem entirely.

7

Usability issues identified across both rounds of testing. 6 of 7 were resolved before the final prototype. 1 issue (date input precision in accessibility mode) was logged as a v2 priority.

AA

WCAG 2.1 AA contrast compliance achieved across all primary text and interactive elements. Minimum contrast ratio of 4.5:1 maintained for body text, 3:1 for large text and UI components.

78
SUS Score
Good

Post-test System Usability Scale (SUS) questionnaires scored an average of 78 out of 100 across 6 participants — placing FamILY in the "Good" range on the standard SUS adjective scale. An 80+ score is considered "Excellent."

The primary driver keeping the score below 80 was the learning curve around the three-mode system. Participants understood each individual mode well, but the concept of switching between modes needed better onboarding. This is the single highest-priority item for a next round of iteration.

The child user in testing gave the Kids Mode a post-session verbal rating of "really easy" and independently completed all assigned tasks — the most encouraging result of the project.

07 — Final Design

The finished prototype — 42 screens across 5 user flows.

The high-fidelity prototype covers onboarding, parent calendar management, event creation with import, Kids Mode, and accessibility mode. All screens were designed at 390×844px (iPhone 14 Pro dimensions).

Key Design Decisions

Three-Mode Architecture

Parent Mode, Kids Mode, and Accessibility Mode share a single data layer. Switching modes changes the UI presentation, not the underlying data — so families see the same information in whatever format works best for them.

Import-First Event Creation

The "Add Event" flow defaults to importing from connected calendars (Google, Apple, Outlook) rather than manual entry — directly addressing the #1 research insight about data-entry fatigue.

Accessibility as a Default

WCAG AA contrast, 56px minimum touch targets, screen reader labels, and reduced-motion options are built in globally — not bolted on as a separate mode. Every screen benefits from these constraints.

08 — Reflection

What I learned. What I'd do differently. What's next.

A 4-week project scope forces prioritization. Here's my honest assessment of what worked, what didn't, and what I'd pursue if this project continued.

What Went Well

Accessibility-first approach paid off

  • Treating accessibility as a constraint from day one — not an add-on — improved the design for every user group, not just accessibility users. The larger touch targets and higher-contrast palette made the app more usable for parents in a hurry too.
  • The three-mode architecture was a structural risk that validated well in testing. Users understood the concept quickly once it was properly surfaced in the navigation.
  • Research genuinely changed my direction. My initial instinct (a simple shared calendar) would have produced a worse product for every user group.

What I'd Do Differently

Better documentation from the start

I under-documented my research process in real time — recruiting method, session protocols, note-taking — which made it harder to articulate the evidence behind decisions later. In my next project, I'll treat documentation as part of the design work, not something to catch up on afterward.

I'd also recruit more diverse participants earlier — specifically users with motor impairments — rather than relying on self-reported accessibility needs from the broader survey group.

What I'd Test Next

Three open questions worth exploring

  • Does the calendar import feature actually reduce event-creation time? A longitudinal diary study over 2 weeks would answer this properly.
  • How do families with 3+ children use the mode-switching? I only tested with 1–2 child households.
  • Does Kids Mode work for a 5-year-old? My child participant was 8. Younger children with pre-literacy needs are a different design challenge entirely.

V2 Priorities

If the project continued

  • Resolve the date-input precision issue flagged in accessibility mode testing (the one finding not addressed in this round)
  • Build out a dedicated onboarding flow for the three-mode concept — the SUS score tells me this is where learnability breaks down
  • Explore AI-powered schedule suggestions ("You have 3 events this weekend — want me to add travel time?") as a power-user feature for Jordan-type parents
  • Conduct a full WCAG audit with a screen reader specialist, not just automated contrast checking

Final Takeaway

Designing for three fundamentally different user types taught me that accessibility constraints make better products for everyone. Every decision made for Marcus made the app easier for Jordan, and more legible for Mia. That's the real case for inclusive design.
