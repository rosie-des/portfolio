01 — Problem

Productivity tools make you efficient. None make you motivated.

Before building anything, I challenged the assumption that a pomodoro timer was a solved problem. Research revealed a gap no existing product had addressed: the emotional dimension of focused work.

The Gap in the Market

"Pomodoro apps are functional but cold. They tell you when to work. None of them remind you why."

Users already relied on YouTube, Pinterest, and Spotify to create motivating study environments — but this meant juggling 4–5 apps, constantly breaking focus. No single product connected their goals to their work sessions.

  • Existing apps (Flocus, Forest, Pomofocus) offer aesthetics but no goal alignment
  • YouTube is the #1 informal pomodoro tool — but it has no timer or productivity layer
  • Personal vision boards (Canva, Pinterest) exist but are completely disconnected from work sessions
  • No app supports multi-modal audio: silence, ambient sound, and motivational content in one place

The Opportunity

"Build a pomodoro tool where your vision board is the background — so every work session is a reminder of what you're working toward."

  • Personal uploads: your Canva vision board, Pinterest imagery, or custom photos
  • YouTube integration: play study vlogs or motivational content in the background
  • Multi-mode audio: deep focus silence, planning energy, subliminal boost
  • Built-in success activities: habit tracker, journaling, to-do lists, motivational quotes
  • One destination — zero app switching

How Might We

How might we create a single, immersive space that combines a productivity timer with personalized, goal-aligned motivation — so users feel connected to their ambitions while they work?

Insight 01

Motivation Is Personal, Not Curated

I initially assumed users would respond to a curated library of aspirational imagery. Research revealed this was wrong — users wanted to use their own vision boards, their own Pinterest boards, their own goals. Generic "success" imagery had the opposite effect for many.

"What if it showed images from my own life and goals instead of someone else's mansion?" — Interview participant, college student, age 21

Insight 02

Audio Mode Matters as Much as Visual

Users clearly distinguished between three types of work sessions with different audio needs: total silence for deep study, verbal/upbeat energy for planning and creative work, and ambient subconscious sounds for flow state. No existing app supported all three.

"For writing, I need music. For math, I need silence. For planning, I want someone talking in the background." — Interview participant, freelancer, age 24

Insight 03

App-Switching Is the Real Productivity Killer

Users reported spending 10–20 minutes "setting up" their study session — opening YouTube, Spotify, Notion, a timer app — before any actual work began. The setup ritual was itself a source of procrastination. Consolidation, not features, was the core value proposition.

"By the time I've got everything open, I've already lost momentum." — Survey respondent, college junior, age 22

Insight 04

The Untapped Niche: Vision Boarding + Productivity

Social media analysis confirmed a significant gap: communities around vision boarding, manifestation, and productivity overlap strongly but no product served both. The audience exists and is actively searching for this combination — it just doesn't exist yet.

"I want the app to feel like it was made for someone like me — ambitious and visual." — Interview participant, age 23

05 — Design Process

From wireframes to a live deployed product.

I moved through the full design and development cycle in two weeks — paper sketches, digital wireframes, usability testing, high-fidelity mockups, and AI-assisted live deployment on Vercel.

01
Paper Wireframes
Sketched 3 core views — Timer, Planning, and Vision Board — to quickly explore layout concepts before committing to pixels. Focused on establishing the sidebar architecture and the relationship between the background media and the timer overlay.
02
Digital Wireframes in Figma
Built low-fidelity digital wireframes establishing the tab navigation (Planning / Syncing / Timer Views), sidebar structure (Visions, Success Activities, Resources), and the core timer module. Used these for the first round of usability testing.
03
Usability Testing — Round 1
Ran moderated sessions with target users. Identified 3 key issues: feature discoverability (sidebar-only access), missing widget component, and need for more customization in vision board management. Iterated design before high-fidelity phase.
04
High-Fidelity Mockups
Applied the full visual design — dark immersive background, frosted timer overlay, the three-mode structure (Deep Focus / Planning / Vision Board). Used Google's color palette for familiarity while pushing toward a more immersive, personal aesthetic.
05
AI-Assisted Development & Deployment
Used AI-assisted vibe-coding tools to build and deploy the live app on Vercel. This was intentional — I wanted to demonstrate that UX designers who can bridge design and code are genuinely rare, and that AI makes this more achievable than ever. The live app is at visipomo.vercel.app.

The Key Design Insight

The biggest structural decision was building three distinct modes (Deep Focus, Planning, Vision Board) that share one background media layer but present completely different UI surfaces. This wasn't in the original wireframes — it emerged from usability testing when users described wanting fundamentally different experiences depending on their current task type.

The sidebar became the "soul" of the app — the place where users connect their goals (vision boards, habit tracker, journals) to their sessions. Every session starts with the question: what are you working toward today?

06 — Usability Testing

Two rounds of testing. Six findings. Every one addressed.

I ran two rounds of moderated usability testing with goal-oriented students and professionals familiar with pomodoro techniques. Participants completed 3 core tasks per session: start a focus session, set up a vision board, and switch audio mode.

2 rounds of testing 5+ participants total 3 tasks per session Moderated remote sessions

Round 1 Findings

Round 1 · Major Features only accessible via sidebar — users missed them entirely

Before

Habit tracking, journaling, and to-do lists were accessible only through the sidebar navigation. During testing, several users completed all three tasks without discovering these features existed — they were too subtle to notice.

Sidebar-only access → users focused on the central timer → secondary features invisible

After

Added a persistent floating action button on the timer view providing quick access to all success activities. Also added contextual nudges during sessions ("Want to log this session in your journal?") to surface features at the right moment.

FAB on timer view + contextual nudges → feature discovery increased significantly in Round 2
Round 1 · Major No desktop widget — users wanted timer access during deep work without the full app

Before

The original design required the full app to be visible to see the timer. Users in deep work sessions wanted a minimal overlay — just the timer countdown — without the immersive environment taking up their full screen.

Full-screen only → forces app to be primary window → incompatible with multitasking workflows

After

Designed a compact desktop widget component showing just the timer and session name. Users can minimize to the widget while working in other apps, keeping the timer visible without interrupting their workflow.

Mini widget component → 90px timer display → session name → break alert → accessible via system tray
Round 1 · Minor Vision board customization too limited — users wanted to organize by goal

Before

The vision board upload system allowed images but no organization — no labels, no reordering, no grouping by goal or theme. Users with multiple goals (career, travel, fitness) wanted to be able to switch between themed boards.

Flat image grid → no labels → no goal grouping → all images mixed together

After

Added board labels, goal-based grouping, drag-to-reorder, and the ability to assign a specific board as the active background. Users can now maintain separate boards for "Career Goals," "Travel," and "Personal Growth."

Named boards → drag reorder → set active board → custom thumbnails → goal-tagged images

Round 2 Findings

Round 2 · Major Cold start confusion — new users didn't know where to begin

Before

First-time users opened the app to a blank immersive environment with no onboarding. They didn't know how to connect their YouTube playlist or upload their vision board before starting — the "empty state" was confusing rather than inviting.

Blank dark screen → no onboarding → users unsure where to start → 2/5 abandoned setup

After

Added a guided 3-step onboarding flow: (1) connect your motivation source (YouTube link or image upload), (2) name your first session, (3) choose your audio mode. Users arrive at their first session feeling set up, not lost.

3-step setup wizard → motivation source → session name → audio mode → arrive ready to work
Round 2 · Minor Audio mode labels unclear — users couldn't choose confidently

Before

The three audio modes were labeled with internal names: "Deep Focus," "Planning Mode," "Subliminal Boost." Users understood the first two but were confused by "Subliminal Boost" — the name didn't communicate what they'd actually hear.

"Subliminal Boost" label → unclear what audio plays → 3/5 users chose wrong mode for their task type

After

Added a one-line description under each audio mode label: "Deep Focus (silence + ambient)" / "Planning Mode (upbeat + verbal)" / "Subliminal Boost (subconscious sounds, no lyrics)." Users reported choosing correctly 100% of the time in validation testing.

Mode name + descriptor line + audio preview icon → confident selection → correct mode chosen every time

09 — Reflection

What I learned about motivation, design, and shipping.

VisiPomo was the first project where I took a design from research all the way through to a live deployed product. That changes how you think about every design decision — you know it's going to be real.

What Went Well

Research changed the product fundamentally

  • My assumption that curated imagery would work was wrong — research revealed personalization was the core value prop. Catching that before building saved enormous rework time.
  • The three-mode architecture (an insight that came from usability testing, not the original spec) became the most-praised aspect of the product in later user feedback.
  • Shipping a live product created a feedback loop beyond the formal study — real users using it for real work sessions provided signals no prototype session could replicate.

What I'd Do Differently

Recruit more diverse users earlier

My research participants skewed toward college students (matching the Jade persona closely). I'd want to test with working professionals, users with ADHD, and older users with different relationships to vision boarding and motivation to stress-test the design beyond its primary audience.

I'd also invest more time in the onboarding flow earlier in the process — the cold start problem that emerged in Round 2 testing should have been anticipated in the initial wireframe phase.

What I'd Test Next

Three open research questions

  • Does having a personal vision board as the background actually improve session quality and goal-achievement over time? This needs a longitudinal study — 4+ weeks of daily use data.
  • What is the optimal session length for the Subliminal Boost audio mode? Standard Pomodoro (25 min) may not be right for this more meditative context.
  • How do users with ADHD experience the immersive environment — does the visual richness help or distract?

V2 Priorities

If the product continues

  • Native Pinterest board sync and Canva import — reduce setup friction to zero
  • AI-generated vision imagery for users who don't yet have a board — immediate emotional investment from first session
  • Mobile companion app — make the motivational environment accessible all day, not just at a desk
  • Session history analytics — make accomplishment data visible and emotionally resonant, not just numerical

Final Takeaway

VisiPomo taught me that motivation is deeply personal — and that the best design insight I had came not from my initial assumptions, but from listening carefully to what users actually needed. The gap between a productive session and an unmotivated one is often just a reminder of why the work matters.
